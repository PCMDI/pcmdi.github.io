Model NCAR (Washington & Meehl): Table Elaborations


 

Participation

This model was originally submitted to the CMIP1 intercomparison, but was subsequently withdrawn from active status at the request of the modeling group representatives.

Spinup/Initialization

The spinup/initialization procedure was as follows (cf. Washington et al. 1994 and Washington and Meehl 1996 for further details):
  • The atmospheric model was initialized from the final quasi-equilibrium state of a run made when coupled to a 50-meter slab mixed-layer ocean without provision of horizontal heat transports (cf. Washington and Meehl 1993).
  • The ocean model was first run for 14 years with robust diagnostic forcing from the three-dimensional observed temperature and salinity fields after Levitus (1982), along with "early winter" SSTs near Greenland specified to produce a meridional overturning of about 20 sverdrup in the Atlantic (cf. Washington et al. 1994). The ocean model then was run for 100 more years with specified surface forcings, but with subsurface forcings turned off. By the end of this integration, the maximum drift in temperature was of the order of a few tenths of a degree near the thermocline.
  • The model atmosphere and ocean then were coupled an integrated for 100 years without application of any flux corrections.

Land Surface Processes

  • Land surface temperature is determined from the balance of surface energy fluxes by the diagnostic method of Holloway and Manabe (1971). (That is, there is no heat diffusion/ storage within the soil.)
  • Soil moisture is represented by the single-layer "bucket" model of Budyko (1956) and Manabe (1969), with field capacity a uniform 0.15 m of water. Soil moisture is increased by both precipitation and snowmelt. It is decreased by surface evaporation, which is determined from the product of the evapotranspiration efficiency beta and the potential evaporation from a surface saturated at the local surface temperature/pressure. Over land, beta is given by the ratio of local soil moisture to the field capacity, with runoff occurring implicitly if this ratio exceeds unity.

Sea Ice

  • Sea ice thermodynamics follow Semtner (1976) with some modifications.  The calculation of the ice/snow thermodynamic state variables is based on the diffusion of heat through the external and internal boundaries of a three-layer system. If the ice thickness is greater than 0.5 m, two layers of ice are maintained; snow, if present, constitutes s a third layer.  When the ice thickness is between 0.25 m and 0.5 m, only a single layer is retained.  If the ice thickness falls below 0.25m, the zero-layer model of Semtner (1976) is employed. The entire net heat flux from the ocean is applied to the lateral ice growth/melt; thus, when computing the vertical balance, the heat flux at the bottom of the ice is set to zero.
  •  Snow accumulation on sea ice is computed from the freshwater flux (precipitation minus evaporation). Any snow melt is added to the freshwater flux into the ocean.  Precipitation on sea ice at the freshwater melting point (0 deg C) is assumed to fall directly into the ocean.
  • A fraction (0.3, assuming cloudy conditions 75 percent of the time) of solar radiation is allowed to penetrate snow-free ice. The penetrating radiation may be transmitted to the ocean or absorbed in the ice, depending on its thickness. The absorbed fraction contributes to the internal melting of ice in brine pockets following Semtner (1976) (i.e., the absorbed flux is stored in a heat reservoir without affecting the overall ice thickness). The ice albedo depends on snow cover/depth and surface temperature.  (The albedo computation assumes a mix of snow-covered and snow-free ice and uses a proxy ‘snow fraction’.)  The albedos of ice and snow depend on spectral interval, and on whether these surfaces are dry or melting.
  • Sea ice dynamics are based on the cavitating-fluid approximation of Flato and Hibler (1990): ice has a finite resistance to compression, but can diverge freely without resistance to shear stress.  For further details, see Washington and Meehl (1996).

Chief Differences from Closest AMIP Model

The atmospheric component of the NCAR (Washington & Meehl) coupled model is a modified Community Climate Model Version Zero A (CCM0A) that is described by Washington and Meehl (1993). Differences from the standard CCM0A model (cf. Washington 1982, Pitcher et al. 1983, Ramanathan et al. 1983, and Malone et al. 1984 ) include the treatment of cloud-radiative interactions, convection, surface characteristics and surface fluxes.

Standard CCM0A was a precursor of the NCAR CCM1 model (cf. Williamson et al. 1987, Kiehl et al. 1987). Except for its explicit inclusion of the radiative effects of greenhouse trace gases, AMIP model SUNYA CCM1-TG (R15 L12) 1990 is identical to the NCAR CCM1. The chief differences between this AMIP model and both the standard and modified (Washington and Meehl 1993) versions of CCM0A are summarized as follows:

    Vertical Representation

    In contrast to the AMIP model, the vertical finite-difference approximations in both the standard and modified (Washington and Meehl 1993) CCM0A models are not energy-conserving.

    Vertical Resolution

    There are 9 vertical levels in the standard and modified (Washington and Meehl 1993) CCM0A models, rather than 12, as in the AMIP model.

    Smoothing/Filling

    In contrast to the AMIP model, the filling of negative values of atmospheric humidity that arise from truncation errors in the discretized moisture equation is not implemented in either the standard or the modified (Washington and Meehl 1993) CCM0A models.

    Diffusion

    • In both the standard and modified (Washington and Meehl 1993) CCM0A models, tropospheric horizontal diffusion is represented in a second-order (del^2) form on sigma surfaces, rather than in fourth-order (del^4) on pressure surfaces, as in the AMIP model. Frictional heating present in the AMIP model also is not included.
    • Vertical diffusion in both the standard and the modified (Washington and Meehl 1993) CCM0A models is linear, and is applied only below 500 hPa, as opposed to the nonlinear diffusion that operates through the entire atmospheric column in the AMIP model. Unlike the AMIP model also, the diffusion coefficient does not depend on local shear and stability.

      •  
    Radiation
    • The modified (Washington and Meehl 1993) CCM0A model follows the standard CCM0A radiation scheme after Ramanathan et al. (1983), except for the specification of cloud shortwave albedo and longwave emissivity. In the Ramanathan et al. (1983) scheme, cloud emissivity is universally a black-body value of unity, while cloud albedo depends on solar zenith angle and cloud height: the albedo is 0.6 for low cloud, 0.3 for middle cloud, and 0.15 for high cloud. In the modified (Washington and Meehl 1993) CCM0A model, middle and high cloud longwave emissivities are set to 0.90 and 0.70 respectively. Middle and high cloud albedos also may increase above their nominal values (0.3 and 0.15, respectively), provided that there is both deep convection and SSTs that are in excess of 303 K; for such conditions, the albedo of middle and high cloud may increase linearly up to 0.6 over an SST range of 303 to 308 K.

      •  
    • Cloud-radiative interactions in the modified (Washington and Meehl 1993) CCM0A model also differ from those of the AMIP model, where stratiform cloud emissivity is a function of diagnostic liquid water content and cloud albedo is a function of optical depth and solar zenith angle, but does not depend on convection or SSTs. Both the standard and the modified (Washington and Meehl 1993) CCM0A models also lack certain other radiative refinements of the AMIP model , such as improved formulations of shortwave absorption by water vapor and oxygen and longwave absorption by water vapor, carbon dioxide, and ozone (cf. Kiehl et al. 1987).

      •  
    • Both the standard and the modified (Washington and Meehl 1993) CCM0A models do not include the radiative effects of trace greenhouse gases that are present in the AMIP model.

      •  
    Convection

    In addition to the moist convective adjustment scheme of the AMIP model (present also in the standard CCM0A model), the modified (Washington and Meehl 1993) CCM0A includes a parameterization of convective mass fluxes of heat and moisture, and a simple cloud model to determine the depth of convection. Cf. Albrecht et al. (1986) and Meehl and Albrecht (1988, 1991) for further details.

    Cloud Formation

    In both the standard and the modified (Washington and Meehl 1993) CCM0A models, atmospheric moisture begins to condense into clouds at 80 percent relative humidity rather than at 100 percent, as in the AMIP model.

    Surface Characteristics

    In contrast to the standard CCM0a model, the sea ice albedo in the modified (Washington and Meehl 1993) CCM0A is a function of latitude and the snow albedo is a function of solar zenith angle; however, these albedos do not depend on spectral interval or surface temperature, as in the AMIP model.

    Surface Fluxes

    The surface wind-stress formulation of the modified (Washington and Meehl 1993) CCM0A model differs from that of the standard CCM0A in the Western Pacific, so as to better reproduce the observations of strong vertical wind shear over this region. The surface drag coefficient in both the standard and modified (Washington and Meehl 1993) CCM0A models is not stability-dependent, as in the AMIP model.
