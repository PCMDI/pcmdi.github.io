Initialization Method (Cf. the review by Stouffer and Dixon 1997 concerning the rationale for the following categories.)

  • 1.  Observational Method
    • A.  Integrate the atmospheric model to equilibrium using prescribed SSTs or by coupling to an oceanic mixed-layer model.
    • B.  Initialize the oceanic model, whole volume, to the observations (e.g., after Levitus), then run a "short time".
    • C.  Couple the atmospheric and oceanic models without flux adjustments.
  • 2.  Reduced Coupling Shock Method
    • A.  Integrate the atmospheric model as in 1A above.
    • B.  Integrate the oceanic model as in 1B above, or start from a constant temperature/salinity distribution with zero currents, and integrate to equilibrium.
    • C.  Take fluxes from 2 A, and integrate the oceanic model to equilibrium by repeating several years of atmospheric fluxes as forcing.
    • D. Either couple the atmospheric and oceanic models and integrate the fully coupled model without flux adjustments, or compute flux adjustments from 2 A and 2 C and integrate the coupled model.

      •  
  • 3.  Coupled Restore Method
    • A.  Integrate the atmospheric model as in 1A above.
    • B.  Integrate the oceanic model as in 1B above, or start from a constant temperature/salinity distribution with zero currents, and integrate to equilibrium.
    • C.  Couple the models from 3 A and 3 B, then integrate the coupled system while restoring the SST, surface salinity, and (optionally) sea ice towards observed values.
    • D.  At some point, stop and compute the flux adjustments (which are the time average of the restoring terms in 2 C above).
    • E.  Continue the coupled integration using the flux adjustments without restoring terms.
    • F.  Possibly, also, iterate steps 3 C, 3D, and 3 E.

      •  
  • 4.  Equilibrium Startup Method
    • A.  Integrate the atmospheric model to equilibrium using prescribed SSTs.
    • B.  Integrate the oceanic model to equilibrium while restoring the SST, surface salinity, and sea ice fields.
    • C.  At equilibrium, compute the flux adjustments from 4 A and 4 B.
    • D.  Couple the models from 4 A and 4 B.  Using the flux adjustments from 4 C, integrate the coupled model.
Run Length: The length of the simulated output data that are archived for the CMIP intercomparison. Note, this run length may be shorter than what actually has been simulated, to date, by the coupled model.

Surface Air Temperature: The definition of each model's "surface air temperature" is summarized. L denotes the temperature of the lowest atmospheric level, with the associated sigma value given in parentheses.  I denotes an air temperature that is intermediate between the temperatures at the ground and at the lowest atmospheric level, with the nominal height noted in parentheses (expressed either in meters above the surface or as an intermediate sigma level).

Missing Data: Data not provided in the CMIP1 requested data list.
